## Reference
- **[12]** A. Kamilaris and F. X. Prenafeta-Boldú. "Deep Learning in Agriculture: A Survey." *Computers and Electronics in Agriculture*, vol. 147, pp. 70–90, Apr. 2018. DOI: 10.1016/j.compag.2018.02.016

## Metadata
- **Year:** 2018
- **Database:** ScienceDirect (Elsevier)
- **Type:** Journal — Survey
- **Q-rank:** Q1 (Computers and Electronics in Agriculture, Elsevier — top agri-tech journal)
- **Citations:** 3,353 (Semantic Scholar) — 68 highly influential | **one of the most cited DL-in-agriculture papers**
- **Affiliation:** Institute for Food and Agricultural Research and Technology (IRTA), Spain

## Keywords
> Deep learning, CNN, convolutional neural network, image processing, pattern recognition, plant disease, weed detection, fruit classification, precision agriculture

## Research Question / Objective
> How has deep learning been applied to agricultural and food production challenges, and does it outperform traditional image processing techniques?

## Methodology
- **Study type:** Survey
- **Papers analyzed:** 40 research efforts
- **Publication date:** April 1, 2018
- **LPWAN technology covered:** Not covered
- **AI/ML technique:** Deep learning — CNN (primary focus), various architectures
- **Agriculture domain:** Plant disease, fruit counting/classification, weed detection, crop type classification, plant recognition

## Key Findings
1. **CNNs dominate** — most promising deep learning architecture for agricultural vision tasks; outperform traditional image processing with occasional exceptions.
2. Application breakdown: fruit/plant classification, plant disease detection, weed detection, crop type classification, fruit counting.
3. Performance is **highly dependent on training dataset quality** — small or unbalanced datasets degrade results significantly.
4. Deep learning achieves high precision and classification accuracy across all covered domains.
5. Data augmentation and transfer learning identified as key strategies to overcome small dataset limitations.

## Limitations Acknowledged by Authors
> - Data preprocessing is time-consuming and necessary
> - High dimensionality + limited training samples in hyperspectral data
> - Incomplete datasets that don't fully describe target problems
> - Lack of publicly available benchmarks for agricultural DL research
> - No LPWAN/IoT integration or real-time deployment assessment

## Relevance to Our Review
- **Section(s) covered:** III (AI/ML Techniques — deep learning subsection), V (Applications — crop/disease/weed)
- **Gap addressed:** Foundational DL-in-agriculture reference — no LPWAN connectivity or IoT sensor integration
- **Citation priority:** **Essential** — 3,353 citations; must cite in Section III alongside Liakos et al. (2018)

## Why It Is NOT a Competitor
> Pure deep learning for agricultural image/vision tasks — no LPWAN, no wireless IoT, no connectivity analysis. Our review integrates the wireless LPWAN data acquisition layer that feeds the AI/ML models this paper surveys.
